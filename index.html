01 · The Problem

Hand-held cameras shake. Software stabilisation crops frames and degrades quality.

Context

Consumer cameras and smartphones produce shaky footage the moment the operator moves. The standard workaround — digital stabilisation — solves it imperfectly: frames get cropped, low-light footage degrades, and fast motion still tears. Mechanical stabilisation eliminates the problem at the source by physically isolating the camera from the operator's motion.

Commercial 3-axis gimbals exist but are expensive, closed systems. We set out to design and build a fully open prototype that handles roll, pitch, and yaw stabilisation in real time, exposes every layer of the stack, and is reproducible with off-the-shelf parts and 3D-printed components.

02 · System Architecture

A closed-loop control system across mechanical, electrical and firmware layers.

The system reads the platform's orientation 100 times a second, computes the correction needed to keep the camera level, and drives three servos to apply that correction — all on a microcontroller small enough to fit inside the handle.

/ MECHANICAL
CAD + 3D-printed frame
Base, pitch elbow, roll elbow and stabilising platform modelled in SolidWorks. Printed in ABS for stiffness, heat resistance and shock tolerance. Mass distribution tuned for a low centre of gravity in the handle.
/ ELECTRICAL
Power + signal layer
Two 7.4V Li-ion cells through an LM2596 buck converter regulated to 5V for the MCU, IMU and servos. PWM lines drive the three MG996R servos directly from the Nano.
/ FIRMWARE
Sensor fusion + control loop
MPU6050's on-chip Digital Motion Processor fuses accel + gyro into quaternions inside a FIFO buffer. Firmware converts to Euler angles and maps them inversely to servo positions.
03 · Controls & Sensor Fusion

Why quaternions — and what gimbal lock actually means in firmware.

The problem

Representing 3D orientation with Euler angles (yaw, pitch, roll) is intuitive but breaks down. When two of the three rotation axes align — a state called gimbal lock — one degree of freedom collapses and the system can no longer distinguish certain rotations. For a stabilisation system that must work in any orientation, this is fatal.

The fix

Orientation is represented internally as a unit quaternion — a four-dimensional rotation primitive that doesn't suffer from gimbal lock and is numerically more stable than rotation matrices. The MPU6050's Digital Motion Processor computes the quaternion onboard and writes it into a FIFO buffer; the firmware reads, converts to yaw–pitch–roll only at the final mapping step, and drives each servo to the inverse of the measured tilt.

// MPU6050 FIFO layout — bytes 0..15 carry the quaternion q = [ w, x, y, z ] // unit quaternion from on-chip DMP // Convert to Euler angles (radians → degrees) only for actuation yaw = atan2(2(wz + xy), 1 − 2(y² + z²)) pitch = asin (2(wy − zx)) roll = atan2(2(wx + yz), 1 − 2(x² + y²)) // Inverse-map to servo targets — gimbal cancels the body's motion servo_yaw = map(yaw, −180, 180, 0, 180) servo_pitch = map(pitch, −90, 90, 180, 0) servo_roll = map(roll, −90, 90, 180, 0)
Calibration

Raw IMU readings drift. Accelerometers pick up linear motion noise; gyroscopes accumulate bias. The firmware runs a PID-based offset calibration at startup, iteratively adjusting accel/gyro offsets until residual error converges to near zero. A complementary filter (98% gyro / 2% accel) is implemented as a fallback path for environments where the DMP isn't enabled, blending the gyro's good short-term response with the accelerometer's good long-term stability.

04 · Build Pipeline

From CAD to a working stabiliser, in twelve steps.

STEP 01
Pilot study & lit review
Surveyed existing gimbal architectures, control strategies, and the trade-offs between mechanical, electrical, and hybrid stabilisation approaches.
STEP 02
Frame design (CAD)
Modelled four mechanical subsystems — base, pitch elbow, roll elbow, stabilising platform — sized to host the camera payload and the electronics inside the handle.
STEP 03
3D printing in ABS
Chose ABS for impact resistance and dimensional stability. Iterated on fits between motor mounts and bearing pivots until joint friction was minimised without play.
STEP 04
Component selection
Specced the MPU6050 (cheap 6-DoF with on-chip DMP), MG996R servos (sufficient torque for a phone-class payload), and Arduino Nano (small, well-supported).
STEP 05
Power architecture
Two Li-ion cells in series at 7.4V, stepped down through an LM2596 buck converter to a regulated 5V rail with thermal shutdown and over-current protection.
STEP 06
Sensor calibration routine
Wrote a PID-driven offset calibration pass that runs at boot, killing the static bias on each accel/gyro axis before the main loop ever starts.
STEP 07
DMP + FIFO integration
Enabled the on-chip Digital Motion Processor, configured the FIFO buffer, and verified packet structure against the i2cdevlib spec.
STEP 08
Quaternion → Euler conversion
Extracted yaw, pitch, and roll from the DMP's quaternion stream and converted to degrees for actuation.
STEP 09
Servo mapping & sign correction
Mapped each axis to the corresponding servo's PWM range; flipped polarity on the axes whose physical mount inverts the rotation direction.
STEP 10
Closed-loop tuning
Tuned filter weights and tested response across walking, running, and sudden-jerk motion profiles. Verified stability on all three axes simultaneously.
STEP 11
Assembly & wiring
Routed power and signal wiring through the gimbal arms, isolating servo current draw from the MCU rail to prevent brownouts during peak torque demand.
STEP 12
Field testing
Captured comparative footage with and without stabilisation, verifying smooth video output across the full range of expected hand motion.

06 · Outcomes

What I took away — and where I'd push it next.

What I built

A working handheld stabiliser that actively cancels operator motion across all three axes. The mechanical, electrical, and firmware layers were designed, integrated, and debugged end-to-end by the four-person team.

What I learned

Closed-loop control systems are unforgiving — every layer (sensor noise, latency, mechanical play, servo dead-band) shows up in the output. Designing for testability from day one matters far more than designing for cleverness. Quaternions feel abstract until gimbal lock breaks your first Euler-based implementation; then they feel essential.

What's next

A magnetometer for absolute yaw reference (the current setup drifts on yaw because there's no gravity vector to anchor it). A Kalman filter to replace the complementary fallback. Wireless remote control for pan/tilt overrides. And a brushless-motor revision — servos are easy but brushless is what every commercial gimbal uses for a reason.
